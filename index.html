Month-by-month planting calendar

Click each month to see what to plant and where. Perennials come back every year; annuals give one season of color.

Last spring frost: ~April 5  ·  First fall frost: ~late October
Tender annuals go outdoors only after last frost. Perennials and shrubs can be planted now.

March
Now

You're in the first prime planting window. Get perennials and shrubs in the ground now so roots establish before summer heat. Amend clay soil with 3–4 inches of compost before planting. Get a free soil test from NC Cooperative Extension (Mecklenburg County).

Last good window to plant annuals and fill gaps. Everything should be in the ground by end of May. Begin a consistent watering schedule as temps climb.

Watering: All new plantings need ~1″ per week. Water deeply 2–3 times weekly, early morning. Avoid wetting leaves on hydrangeas. Second hydrangea fertilizer application (10-10-10).
June
Maintain & enjoy

Your garden is blooming. Focus shifts to deadheading, watering, and pest patrol. Hydrangeas should be flowering in Zones C and D.

Deadhead spent blooms
Knockout roses
Snip faded clusters. They’ll rebloom within 5–6 weeks.
A
Coneflowers & daylilies
Remove spent blooms to encourage continuous flowering.
ACE
Pest watch: Japanese beetles arrive mid-June. Hand-pick or use neem oil on roses and coneflowers. Check hydrangea leaves for leaf spot — water at root zone only.
July
Maintain & enjoy

Peak summer heat. Keep watering, keep deadheading. Final hydrangea feeding of the year. Cut back any leggy perennials by a third — they’ll push fresh growth and rebloom in late summer.

Final fertilizer: Apply 10-10-10 to hydrangeas (last of the Mar/May/Jul schedule). Stop all fertilizing after July — late feeding promotes tender growth that won’t harden before winter.
Water deeply: Charlotte July heat can be brutal. New plantings need 1–2″ per week. Mulch helps retain moisture.
August
Maintain & enjoy

Hottest, driest month. Don’t let new plantings suffer. Start planning fall additions and order spring-blooming bulbs from catalogs — they ship in fall.

Replace tired annuals
Fresh lantana or zinnias
Swap out any exhausted marigolds or impatiens with fresh transplants.
AE
Order bulbs now: Daffodils, tulips, crocuses, hyacinths. They’ll arrive in September/October for fall planting.
September
Fall planting

The second (and arguably best) planting window. Roots establish through winter without heat stress. Fall is ideal for expanding your perennial beds and adding shrubs.

October
Fall planting

The big bulb planting month. Everything you plant now will be your first burst of spring color. Last good window for perennial shrubs too.

Spring-blooming bulbs to plant
Daffodils
Plant 6–8″ deep. Clusters of 5–7. Naturalize and return every year. Deer-proof.
ACE
Tulips
Plant 6–8″ deep. Many colors. Treat as annuals in Zone 7b — may not reliably return.
AC
Crocuses
First flowers of spring. Plant 3–4″ deep in drifts. Tiny but cheerful.
ACE
Hyacinths
Intensely fragrant. Plant 6″ deep. Blue, pink, or white.
AC
Refresh mulch to 2–3″ before winter. This insulates roots and keeps soil temperature stable through freeze/thaw cycles.
November
Wind down

Clean up spent summer annuals and add them to compost. Leave perennial seed heads standing — coneflower and black-eyed Susan seed heads feed goldfinches through winter.

Last bulb window
Daffodils & crocuses
You can still plant spring bulbs through late November in Mint Hill.
ACE
Leave the seed heads: Don’t cut back coneflowers, black-eyed Susans, or bee balm stalks. Their seed heads are winter food for songbirds.
