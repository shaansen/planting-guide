Frost dates for Charlotte/Mecklenburg County (NOAA NCEI data)

Last spring frost (32°F): Average April 2 (NC State Extension). 50% probability by April 11; safe at 80% confidence after April 11 (Garden.org/NOAA).

First fall frost (32°F): Average November 5 (NC State Extension). Frost risk begins around October 22; 50% probability by November 3 (Garden.org/NOAA).

Growing season: ~April 1 to November 11, roughly 225 days (WCNC/NWS). Charlotte has never recorded a freeze before October (earliest: October 9, 2000).

Sources: NC State Extension (Mecklenburg County frost chart); Garden.org frost calculator (NOAA NCEI data); WCNC Charlotte (NWS frost/freeze program).

Best time to plant trees in Charlotte: During the dormant season, from fall after leaves drop through early spring before buds break (TreesCharlotte). Fall (mid-September through early December) is ideal — cooler air, warm soil, and more rainfall create perfect root-establishment conditions (NC Cooperative Extension; NC Arboretum). Spring (March–early April) also works if fall is missed.
Best: October – December (dormant season)
Ideal window

Trees planted now focus energy on root development through winter. Warm soil persists into December in Charlotte. Davey Tree experts suggest planting between Thanksgiving and Christmas as the “sweet spot” (WCNC). NC Cooperative Extension recommends mid-September through early December.

Good: February – March (late dormant / early spring)
Also works

If you missed the fall window, early spring before buds break is the next best time. Plant before new growth starts, typically by late March. All the same trees listed above apply. Container-grown trees from nurseries can be planted now.

Spring planting note: Trees planted in spring have less time to establish roots before summer heat. Water deeply and consistently through the first summer — at least 1–2″ per week. Mulch 2–3″ around the base (keep mulch away from the trunk).
All trees above are frost-hardy and can be planted while frost is still possible. Unlike tender annual flowers, trees are dormant during this period and are not harmed by freezing temperatures.
Avoid: June – August (summer heat)
Not recommended

Avoid planting trees during peak summer heat. Charlotte’s hot, humid summers cause transplant stress, and trees struggle to establish roots while also pushing new growth. If you must plant in summer, water extremely frequently and provide temporary shade for small trees.

March

First prime planting window. Get perennials and shrubs in the ground now so roots establish before summer heat. Amend clay soil with 3–4 inches of compost. Get a free soil test from NC Cooperative Extension (Mecklenburg County).

Frost still likely through early April. Charlotte’s average last frost is April 2 (NC State Extension), with an 80% chance of frost still occurring as late as April 1 (Garden.org/NOAA). Only plant frost-hardy perennials and shrubs this month. Do not plant tender annuals yet.

April
Prime planting

The frost window is closing. Continue planting perennials. After mid-April, frost-tender annuals can go outdoors. Apply slow-release 10-10-10 fertilizer to all beds.

Early April: frost still possible. Average last frost is April 2 but the 50% probability mark is April 11 (NOAA data). Wait until after April 11 to plant frost-tender annuals outdoors, and watch the forecast. If a surprise frost threatens, cover tender plants with sheets overnight.

Maintain & enjoy

Your garden is blooming. Focus on deadheading, watering, and pest patrol. Hydrangeas should be flowering in Zones C and D. No frost concerns.

Pest watch: Japanese beetles arrive mid-June in the Charlotte area. Hand-pick or use neem oil on roses and coneflowers. Check hydrangea leaves for leaf spot — water at root zone only.
July
Maintain & enjoy

Peak summer heat. Keep watering, keep deadheading. Final hydrangea feeding of the year. Cut back leggy perennials by a third — they’ll push fresh growth and rebloom in late summer.

Final fertilizer: Apply 10-10-10 to hydrangeas (last of the Mar/May/Jul schedule). Stop all fertilizing after July — late feeding promotes tender growth that won’t harden before the first frost in November.
Water deeply: Charlotte July heat can be brutal. New plantings need 1–2″ per week. Mulch helps retain moisture.
August

Hottest, driest month. Don’t let new plantings suffer. Start planning fall additions and order spring-blooming bulbs from catalogs — they ship in fall.

October
Fall planting

The big bulb planting month. Everything you plant now becomes your first burst of spring color. Last good window for perennial shrubs too.

Frost risk begins late October. According to Garden.org/NOAA data, there is a 20% chance of a 32°F frost by October 27. Frost-tender annuals (zinnias, lantana, impatiens, caladiums, coleus) will be killed by the first freeze. Enjoy them while they last, and let frost-hardy pansies and mums take over.

November
Wind down

Clean up spent summer annuals killed by frost and add them to compost. Leave perennial seed heads standing — coneflower and black-eyed Susan seed heads feed goldfinches through winter.

First freeze typically arrives now. Average first frost is November 5 (NC State Extension). There is a 50% chance of a 32°F freeze by November 3 and 80% by November 12 (Garden.org/NOAA). The average first freeze is November 8 with a typical window of November 1–10 (WCNC/NWS). All frost-tender annuals will be dead after the first hard freeze.
